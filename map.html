PFAS Contamination in Florida Drinking Water

By Carolina Ilvento

At least 4.5 million Floridians have their household's tap water contaminated with PFAS, or per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, at levels higher than suggested by the Environmental Protection Agency.

Some facilities have detected levels as high as nearly 700 ppt — over 175 times higher than the proposed safe limit. The EPA proposed a standard of 4 parts per trillion of PFOA and PFOS combined, as well as and a hazard index of four other PFAS in drinking water.

Although the EPA's standards are not yet finalized, the Unregulated Contaminants Monitoring Rule (UCMR) mandates that public water systems serving more than 3,300 residents must test for four types of PFAS every five years.

The latest data, released in July, revealed PFAS detection in 33 water systems across the state.

In addition to these mandated tests, certain facilities in Florida have exercised their discretion to conduct additional testing, both in terms of frequency and comprehensiveness.

Researchers from various institutions across the state have also been studying the presence of PFAS in soil, water, wildlife, and even in the food supply.

Dr. John Bowden, a researcher and professor at the University of Florida, has dedicated several years to understanding the presence and risks associated with PFAS. In his most recent study, Dr. Bowden collected tap water samples from 460 households spanning every county in Florida, conducting tests for 24 different types of PFAS.

The study's results revealed the highest PFAS detections in Broward, Escambia, Hamilton, Indian River, Miami-Dade, Monroe and Okeechobee Counties, with contamination levels ranging from 50 ppt to 219 ppt.

What the Data Might Hide

With ongoing research into PFAS contamination in Florida's drinking water and developing guidelines and regulations for the chemical, it's important to acknowledge the existing data gaps and limitations in understanding the findings of these complex chemicals.

Some of the notable data gaps include:

1. Limited Sampling Sites:

Data does not include every water source in the state. Due to a lack of enforced standards, not every facility has conducted testing or made it public, so it's possible that some PFAS-contaminated areas remain undetected.

2. Variability in PFAS Types and Lack of Regulations:

PFAS are a broad class of chemicals with thousands of variants, and not all are tested for. While some facilities test for numerous PFAS varieties, others test just a few or none at all. This inconsistency creates data gaps and may lead to variations in reported results that don't accurately represent the actual PFAS concentration.

3. Infrastructure Issues:

While treatment plants may efficiently reduce PFAS concentrations in water, trace contaminants can persist as water travels through distribution systems and ultimately reaches consumers' taps. Factors like treatment process effectiveness, infrastructure condition, and contamination source can contribute to differences in PFAS exposure between plants and households.


Methodology

The information on this page was compiled from public records requests sent to water treatment facilities across the state, information gathered as part of the EPA's Unregulated Contaminants Monitoring Rule as of July 5, 2023, and a forthcoming study on PFAS in tap water by Dr. John Bowden from the University of Florida.

Public Records Requests were made to water treatment facilities for service areas and most recent PFAS testing. Information on map and graphs provided display highest detections in water treatment facilities.

Service areas had common areas in some facilities and were not provided by others. For areas with multiple facilities serving the same area, information is included from all relevant facilities that might attend the area and highest detection results are displayed. In places where service areas were not specified, data will reflect information from facilities in the same county.

In the event that your location does not have testing results from facilities, check your water bill for your water provider's information and request their latest PFAS testing results. It is possible that testing has not been conducted, as it is not mandated by the Environmental Protection Agency as of October 2023.

All tap water data was provided by Bowden, who tested tap water in 460 households across Florida. Specific locations are not disclosed to protect homeowners' privacy. The text shows the number of household samplings in your service area or county and represents the highest recorded PFAS level.

Data and analysis was informed by interviews and discussions with the water treatment testing supervisors, Environmental Working Group advisors and Bowden. Data was updated Oct. 20, 2023.
